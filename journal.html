Beautiful and Useful

Why everyday things deserve to be both practical and lovely.

I've always loved the idea that everyday things should be both beautiful and useful.

Not precious objects hidden away for "best", and not purely practical things that make a home feel cold or temporary — but ordinary objects that quietly do their job while still bringing some kind of pleasure to the room.

I first came across the phrase through the potter Bernard Leach, who was influenced by the Japanese philosopher and thinker Yanagi Sōetsu and the wider Mingei movement — the idea that beauty can exist in simple, honest, handmade everyday objects. A bowl, a jug, a woven basket, a linen towel, a wooden stool. Things made to be used properly, not just admired.

I think homes feel calmer when the useful things are beautiful enough to leave out. A kitchen feels softer when the chopping board and cast iron pan are things you genuinely enjoy looking at. A simple lamp can change the atmosphere of a room completely if it gives off a warm light and feels considered.

The older I get, the less interested I am in perfect interiors. I don't want rooms that feel staged or untouched. I like homes that feel lived in gently — where useful things have earned their place, and where beauty comes from texture, light, practicality and care rather than perfection.

I think that's why I'm drawn to natural materials so often: wood, linen, ceramic, woven baskets, cotton, glass, greenery. They age well, soften spaces and somehow make everyday routines feel slower and more grounded.

For me, beautiful and useful isn't really a decorating style. It's more a way of thinking about the home. Choosing fewer things, but choosing them carefully. Letting practical objects become part of the atmosphere instead of trying to hide them away.

The best homes rarely feel the most expensive. They just feel thoughtful.
